# Concept

## Concise explanation

Living Legal Systems model law as a graph of rules and interpretations. Statutes, amendments, cases, opinions, codifications, commentaries, and jurisdictional adaptations all become linked objects, making legal development navigable as lineage rather than only text search.

## Problem being solved

Legal systems evolve through layered references, but those references are usually spread across disconnected repositories and institutional silos. It is hard to see how a present rule depends on prior cases, revisions, and competing interpretations.

## Why ledger-native architecture helps

- precedent and amendment chains become inspectable and versioned
- jurisdictional forks can preserve ancestry instead of obscuring divergence
- commentary, translations, and summaries can remain linked to source authority
- public legal memory can outlast any one publisher or database provider

## Future expansion ideas

- civic interfaces that explain legal change over time
- machine-assisted precedent maps for judges, advocates, and researchers
- public goods funding for maintaining high-quality legal graphs
